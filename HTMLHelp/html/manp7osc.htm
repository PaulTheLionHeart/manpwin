Oscillators and Attractors or Maps or Dynamic Systems

Dynamic systems are continuous, such as oscillators, or discontinuous, such as maps and represent physical and biological phenomena but do not have images of those phenomena.

This section is divided into 2 parts: 1. Suggestions and procedure and 2. Explanations and Justification

1.     Suggestions and Procedure

Choose the flowing menu options: Fractals; Fractal Type; Marcus Contributions; Attractors from Oscillators; for example, choose Aizawa Chaotic Attractor and press ‘ok’. Or alternatively choose another oscillator among more than 1000 options. Naming of the oscillators is based on the papers used as a reference. They also supplied the equations, constants and initial values of the variables, when available.

A 2 dimensional image (an attractor) of a 3 or more dimensions oscillator is displayed and several options are available, for example, Morphing as follows.

2.     Explanations and Justification

 

Explanation about the Number of Variables

Using the present and the past position of each variable, ManpWIN doubles the number of variables. The time difference is specified by the parameter ‘dt’. The past values are available when calculating the present values, so no new differential equations are needed to increase their number; consequently there is no additional processing time. In the papers, a 3D oscillator produces three 2D and one 3D attractors. ManpWIN, using 6 variables for this same oscillator, produces fifteen 2D or twenty 3D attractors. The majority of them are not supposed to exist except if we are considering ‘gradients’ of the variables.

 

What is morphing

ManpWIN first calculates all 2D attractors of an oscillator. All of them have the same number of dots (or lines). Here we have a perfect condition to make smooth, linear transitions from one to the subsequent attractor like in a movie. Besides exposing at once all attractors of an oscillator to a visiting person. Our internal use has been to choose the most different, beautiful or exquisite 2D attractor to be the default image of this oscillator.

 

Why or `what is this?`,  the use other coordinate systems

The developers of ManpWIN, accidently overlooked that an oscillator was described by  and plotted it as (x, y, z). The images generated didn`t match the published images but nevertheless were beautiful. Returning the program the oscillator as  we returned to the original images of the paper. That was ‘the seed’ to implement other 11 coordinate systems for all oscillators in ManpWIN. It allows all operations on those ‘mathematical attractors’ as if they were real-world attractors.

 

What is evolution?

The sequence of the construction of a 3D attractor is displayed over time.

 

What Rotation gives

Rotation enables all sides of a 3D attractor to be seen. This operation is valid for every coordinate system used, but changing the order of the ordinates may cause different spatial images, except for the Cartesian coordinate system.

 

What the ‘P’ command gives

This tool was implemented for all authors using ManpWIN, to refine in their papers, any or all constants or variables to get what they would like, visualised by ManpWIN`s images. The options can be chosen in the dialogue box ‘Parameter Number to be animated`; and the lower and upper bounds in the lateral boxes. Press `ok’.

Surfaces, Knots and Curves

Surfaces, Knots and Curves are mathematical entities that do not depend on differential equations and are ‘simple’ functions of two or three variables; but to calculate their length, area, and surface, they need to use of that resource.

For operations on Rotation, Evolution, ‘P’ function, Morphing, consult this Help in the section of Attractors and Maps.

Other points like Resizing, Zooming, coloring, in the main page, common to fractals, there are a lot of options.

1.     One important thing that was new for Surfaces and that has been was to draw the front of the surfaces, covering entirely the back part of the surfaces.

2.     Knots, for us familiar with lines a simplest idea came: to draw circles instead of dots sufficiently touching to show a continuous ‘tube’ when we could shows what was covering what and give a 3D notion also reinforced by colours en dégradée.

3.     Curves were a next interrogation we made: “why not?” type of questions to be solved, in particular, one by one, was to define scales and limits of infinite curves.

4.     Note that morphing is not available for surfaces because this operation occurs totally in RAM and there are too many points contained in a surface to allow the morphing process to occur.

 

Some final comments

The morphing operation in ManpWIN was implemented for four reasons:

1.     There are no other situation in the literature where morphing could be more exact and perfect: during the operation, all millions of points from the initial image (image 1) have total and complete bi-univocal correspondence (1:1) till arriving to image 2. The transitions have the same number of point because we do that morphing via a linear operator.

2.     As said above, the intermediate positions (Instantons) during the transition are also attractors, billions, trillions of them that can be plotted through equations conjuring the initial and final attractors via a linear connection.

3.     It was another provision for the fans and users of ManpWIN that have now another kind of dynamic presentations of oscillators they could find in no other places be in literature of software.

4.     A proof of our creativity and problem solver, both instances that we are very proud of.

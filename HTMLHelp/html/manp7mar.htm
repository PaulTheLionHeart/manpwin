Marcus Fractals:

These are contributions to ManpWIN from Marcus Rezende who is an early pioneer in fractal exploration.

Attractors from Oscillators and Fractal Maps from Oscillators:

Naming of the Chaotic Oscillators and Fractal Maps hasn’t been easy because a simple change of variable can create an entirely new fractal. Where possible, we have given credit to the main authors of these fractals as best as we could.

Marcus came up with an interesting colouring scheme for Fractal Maps. This is the default. However to get the “raw” colour, uncheck “Use Special Colouring” on the Fractal Map selection dialogue box.

Many of these have co-ordinates in 3 or 4 dimensions and can therefore be viewed along any axis. Where possible, i.e. where there are at least 3 dimensions and the entire fractal is contained within the screen co-ordinates, animation is possible through 360 degrees.

Each oscillator or fractal map can be viewed in the following co-ordinate systems: Cartesian, Spherical, Cylindrical, Parabolic, Paraboloidal, Elliptical, Bipolar, Toroidal, Prolate Spheroidal, Oblate Spheroidal, Bispherical or Bipolar Cylindrical, effectively increasing the number of possible images created by a factor of 12.

 

Two methods are used to determine the centre of rotation. The main method is to only view the points within a specific volume. Sometimes it is better to find the centre of mass by taking the statistical average for each rotation axis. These two methods can be selected by the user.

 

Because there are 2 variable sets that can produce interesting images, the number of possible axes is 2 times the dimensions of the oscillators.  For example, a 4 dimensional oscillator will have a possible 8 axes that can be used for display. In order to simplify entry of axes to a single number, the dimensions for x, y and z axes are entered XYZ, where these can be 1 - 9 or A-Z representing numbers 10 – 35. For example, if the user wants to display dimensions 4, 13, 17 then 4DH is entered. Refer to Fractal Animation.

Refer also to Comments on Oscillators, Knots and Surfaces by the main contributor, Marcus Rezende.

Marcus also interpreted knots by adding a time dimension to them and these can be viewed as oscillators. They change from static to dynamic structures. Their name is prefixed with a tilde “~” to separate them from regular oscillators and they appear at the end of the list of oscillators.

Strange Attractors:

These are additional Maps from oscillators based on the book by Julien C. Sprott, “Strange Attractors: Creating Patterns in Chaos”. The naming conventions introduced in the book are kept as well as the code strings that are interpreted as coefficients of the polynomials.

This is implemented in ManpWIN with the following code:

length = (int)strlen(s);

for (j = 0; j < length; j++)

    a[j] = (double)(*(s + j) - 65.0) / 10.0 - 1.2;

 

where s is the string with the characters that represent the coefficients and a[j] is the array of coefficients produced.

Not all attractors are included as some have up to 504 coefficients.

Knots:

An amazing array of knots has been found on Jürgen Meier’s site http://www.3d-meier.de/. These may be rotated an evolved similarly to oscillators. Knots are plotted using small circles and their diameter can be varied by the user. Each knot can be projected into the same co-ordinate system as specified in the oscillator section.

Refer also to Comments on Oscillators, Knots and Surfaces by the main contributor, Marcus Rezende.

Surfaces:

These are mainly taken from Jürgen Meier’s site http://www.3d-meier.de/, but a few are also taken from Paul Bourke’s site http://paulbourke.net/. There is a trade-off between generation time and quality of the surface texture by varying the number of iterations. This is important for animation, depending on the number of frames being created. Similar to oscillators, centres of rotation may be generated in two ways depending on the distribution of the points on the surface. Each surface can be projected into the same co-ordinate system as specified in the oscillator section.

Refer also to Comments on Oscillators, Knots and Surfaces by the main contributor, Marcus Rezende.

Curves:

These are mainly taken from Jürgen Meier’s site http://www.3d-meier.de/, as the cross sections for surfaces. Some are also taken from Paul Bourke’s site http://paulbourke.net/. Others were found on https://www.mathcurve.com, http://mathworld.wolfram.com, http://www-groups.dcs.st-andrews.ac.uk/~history/Curves/Curves.html as well as other sites.

 

The 2 dimensional curves have an artificially created z axis to help with 3D animation. These are also able to be translated into other co-ordinate systems.

 

Malthus Population Growth:

A curious formula appeared in an old Pascal book with one image quite different from the common 2D bifurcation fractal. As a novice in fractal programming, one of us made a Basic code in his MSX computer (something between ´85 and ´88).  This formula was tested successively in a ´286, ´386, ´486, until the i7, and works. Meanwhile lateral changes made different images as here are presented as a debut.

Main formulae:

p = rate * (p0 * (1 - p0))

x = p0 * p

y=x0

Changing the 'rate' default, may or may not get a better image because this program is very sensitive for this variable, but the signal change can bring new images!!

Increasing the 'Outer Loop Counter' can make differences (and longer processing time also).

Increasing the 'Inner Loop Counter'   we can have the image with many more points (with a higher definition of the image) in a longer time, of course.

Some images seem deceptive but all have their charm ...and bifurcations.

Try negative rates, such as -2.3, for more interesting fractals.

Escher-Sierpinski:

These fractals have their double name for obvious reasons: Sierpinski is the construction mode. Decreasing the sizes of the components as you go to the exterior or interior margin of the fractal gets its name from M. C. Escher had made in many of his works of art.

Many people have done something similar but we extrapolate their presentations in many ways.

The images normally do not have good resolution because we see the individual components (that are fractals in themselves) and if we go to a state of higher 'Density', the individual components disappear because a fuzzy interpenetration prevails.

The user can choose numbers where he/she can attain a compromise solution.

The fractals are grouped:

1.      Normal polygon full of fractalettes: 'a' - 'e', 'l' - 'o', 'u' and 'v'

2.      Flake polygon 'f' - 'h', 'j' - 'k', intermingled borders from the pentagon on

3.      Multiple polygons 'p', 'r' - 's'

4.      Disperse polygons 'q' and 't' - the fractal in concentrated at the center and vanishes without definite borders. Pay attention to the center.

5.      Types 'm' - 'o' are the main responsive for the inclusion of the name “Escher" for this group of fractals.

The formulae are complex and change for each option.

Sierpinski Flowers:

This is based on the standard Sierpinski gasket.

What is new is the generic formula that merges the image size, the number of sides and distance of the flakes (petals) in the shape of a given polygon from 3 to 100 sides.

The Sierpinski method of construction is always an inspiration.

Stars and Polygons:

The fractal "Mondrian" from the ´87 book: "Fractals, Images of Chaos" of Hans Lauwerier was the inspiration that we took freely to make the stars, lines and polygons.

It is based on random generating points to be center of random sized polygons in random directions and of random colors but with a fixed, chosen number of sides.

The stars have the same pseudo-chaotic generation but with a fixed, chosen number of arms.

The vertical/horizontal lines are almost the same as in the book but with a coloring.

Ikeda Attractor:

This fractal is one of many Strange Attractors but has a justification specific to physics and "has a name", something that makes it distinctive among an infinite, chaotic population. It is observed as a "primus inter paris" in many internet sites and so it more than deserves to be part of the ManpWIN fractal collection. It has been moved to “Maps from Oscillators” but is mentioned here because of the unique animation method. Refer to the Animation Menu.

Cross Roads:

This exquisite and original fractal gets its name not only by the initial 4 main crossing-lines figure but also by the generous ways it leads us near to the beautiful vicinages of Lyapunov, Volterra-Lotka, Phoenix, FrothyBasin. Beware: there is a lot of dust along our unpaved routes. Perhaps that´s what is misleading our imagination, dust falling in our eyes.

Basic formulas:

 

x = y0 - (x0 + y0 / ABS(x0)) / (ABS(b * x0 + y0))

y = a + c - x

Some Subtypes options (6, for instance) seems saturated with points but we can resolve that by using an ‘exponent' = .1 or less, in conjunction or not with a negative 'a'.

Subtype 2, with a negative 'a' requires a greater 'exponent' to get a less diffuse image.

Try all the options this fractal and zoom in to see rivulets, grasses, and more rainbows. No warranty or refund whatsoever, if those conditions are not found !!!

Numeric Triangles:

The inspiration for this fractal came from: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_fractals_by_Hausdorff_dimension, where we could see the Pascal Triangle modulo 3and modulo 5, among other inspirations.

From this point on we found (in the internet) numbers that were obtained in a dual entrance conditions and that had a triangular conformation when listed by lines.

We could find such numbers named by: Bell, Catalan, Dudley, Floyd, Hosoya, Liebniz, Lozanic and the 2 Stirlings, which we manage to display here (we do not know if they have their Hausdorff dimension calculated. Any advice is greatly appreciated).

We invite you too to send information (and formulae) of other similar not included here.

Apollonian Circles:

The majority of fractal sites on the internet show an Apollonian circle or 'his' gasket. Hence, it is appropriate for this fractal to be part of the ManpWIN collection of fractals. Two slide controls allow the user to define the size of the two main circles.

This program currently allows the possibility to make the 'collar' and the objectives’: a "gasket of two" and "gasket of three" equal main circles, but currently does not allow the “gasket of four” - it begins to fragment. This may be addressed in a future release.

There are 3 filling options: hollow, filled and solid.

Zigzag Spirals:

These are not a pure fractal, a protofractal as Leuwerier (opus cit.) call his spirals but have a look-like construction and formulae. Leuwerier states: "Spirals have become the building blocks of fractals"   "'...also the building blocks of nature".

Basic formulae:

x = x0 * cos(a / k1) +/- (y0 + x0 ^ exp 1) * sin(a)

y = x0 * sin (a) +/- (x + x0 ^ exp 2) * cos(a)

These formulae were obtained purely incidentally working with similar formulae. Once we found the first crooked spiral, the name ZigZag emerged and thus the inspiration to search all its potential for new shapes, not only for the lines in themselves but also (sometimes chiefly) about its mid-point dancing movement - which is a new, central point of view.

Mec-Spider’s Web:

Mec-Spider´s web is a mistake that had its origin in the Chua´s fractal.

Trying to implement it without the derivatives operations, using only the equations and constants resulted in a chaotic triangles distribution with concentrated centres of diminutive triangles.

The many images produced made the “Lévy Flight” images to came into consideration (not the statistical distributions associated with it).

Spiders also do make clouds of mini-webs like that, what may be considered that it’s a food procedure, make more probable a happy hunting, instead of walking for food.

So, Chua´s look-alike equations, Lévy Flight image semblance, spider’s web distribution in nature, all of them together made difficult for us to name this random fractal.

Mec-Spider is for Mechanical-Spiders, because alive ones would be spinning more direct lines between the centres of those spaced small webs.

If anyone would like to analyse and describe this distribution, we can send the equations and so he/she can work with the pairs of numbers produced at each loop.
